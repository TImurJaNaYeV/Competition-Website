# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Ambitious high school students aged 14–18 across Central Asia (Kazakhstan, Russia, Uzbekistan) and internationally, with genuine interest in pursuing careers in business, finance, and economics. They are academically competitive, internationally minded, and self-selected for ambition — they follow Bloomberg, care about university admissions, and compete in olympiads. They arrive at this site having heard about the championship through school networks or peers, and are evaluating whether to commit to registering. Parents and school administrators are secondary readers who need to feel the event is legitimate and well-run.

## Product Purpose

The BEF Championship website exists to convert curious, ambitious students into registered competitors. The site must communicate prestige and seriousness — this is a real championship that will challenge them and advance their careers — while making registration feel accessible and exciting, not bureaucratic. Success means a student lands on the homepage, feels the pull of the event's quality, and clicks Register Now.

## Brand Personality

Sharp, editorial, competitive. The brand carries the confidence of a financial institution, the intellectual credibility of a university, and the energy of a high-stakes hackathon. Voice: direct, precise, zero filler. Tone: ambitious but welcoming — this is for the best, and you could be among them.

## Anti-references

- Generic SaaS landing pages: teal-on-white gradients, floating blobs, "modern startup" aesthetic
- Corporate consulting navy: deep navy + gold as a cliché of prestige
- School/EdTech friendly-rounded look: pastel palettes, rounded pill buttons, "approachable" type
- Cookie-cutter hackathon: neon purple/cyan on dark, glitch effects, circuit-board motifs
- Bloomberg terminal literal: monospace-everything, pure utility with no warmth at all

## Design Principles

1. **One stage, one spotlight.** Every section has a single focal point. No competing hierarchies; the eye is always led somewhere specific.
2. **Prestige through restraint.** Quality signals come from proportion, spacing, and precision — not from decoration. Fewer elements, bolder choices.
3. **Editorial confidence.** Typography does the heavy lifting. Headlines are statements, not suggestions. The type is not friendly; it is authoritative.
4. **Motion with intention.** Transitions reveal information; they don't decorate it. Nothing animates unless it earns its moment.
5. **Designed for the ambitious.** This audience reads Bloomberg and watches MUN. They have taste. The design should respect that — never condescend, never oversimplify.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA. All body text ≥4.5:1 contrast. Large/bold headings ≥3:1. Visible focus indicators on all interactive elements. Full keyboard navigation. `prefers-reduced-motion` respected throughout — crossfade or instant transitions as fallback. Bilingual (English / Russian) — all strings in translations.js; layout must not break under Russian text which runs 20–40% longer.
